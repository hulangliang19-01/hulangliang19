abstract: "Kobo Abe's The Woman in the Dunes appears at first to be a narrative of captivity and escape, yet its ending unsettles the assumption that freedom simply means leaving the sand pit. This essay argues that the novel stages freedom as a paradox produced by punishment. Its primary framework combines Michel Foucault's account of discipline with Lauren Berlant's account of attachment: the pit trains Jumpei's body, while the water device gives him a reason to remain within the damaging world that has trained him. Material ecocriticism and posthuman theory sharpen this argument by showing how sand, insects, water, skin, and dead specimens participate in the production of subjectivity. Abe's central question is not simply whether Jumpei can escape, but how freedom can be distinguished from an attachment produced by the very environment that confines him."

summary: "This essay reads Kobo Abe's The Woman in the Dunes as a philosophical and material inquiry into freedom, discipline, and attachment."
